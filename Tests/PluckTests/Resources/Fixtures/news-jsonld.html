Mars Rover Sends Back New Images

NASA's Perseverance rover transmitted a new batch of high-resolution photographs from the Jezero crater this morning, providing the most detailed look yet at a fluvial delta system formed billions of years ago. Mission scientists say the images reveal layered sediment patterns consistent with sustained water flow.

The rover, which landed in February 2021, has been steadily exploring the crater's western edge. Its instruments include high-resolution stereo cameras, a ground-penetrating radar, and a sample-collection system that will eventually return Martian rock and soil to Earth as part of a future mission.

According to the project lead, the latest data set will inform decisions about which samples to cache for return to Earth. The team is particularly interested in the boundary between two distinct rock layers, where evidence of ancient microbial life might be preserved if it ever existed on the planet.

Perseverance has now been operating on Mars for more than five years, far exceeding its original primary-mission lifetime. Engineers continue to find creative workarounds for hardware degradation, allowing the rover to keep producing science daily despite mounting wear on its drive system and sample arm.
